**LLM-as-a-judge** is a technique where you use a language model to **evaluate the outputs of another model (or system)** instead of relying only on humans or rigid metrics.

A senior framing:

> LLM-as-a-judge = using an LLM as a **scalable evaluator that approximates human judgment for quality, correctness, and safety.**

---

# 1. Why it exists

In real LLM systems, many things are hard to measure with exact metrics:

* “Is this answer helpful?”
* “Is this reasoning correct?”
* “Is this hallucinated or grounded?”
* “Which answer is better?”

These are **semantic + subjective problems**, so rule-based metrics fail.

---

# 2. Core idea

You give a judge model:

* a prompt
* model output(s)
* sometimes reference context or ground truth
* a rubric

And it returns a **score or ranking**.

---

## Example

### Input:

```text id="i1"
Question: What is FAISS?
Answer: FAISS is a library for vector similarity search.
```

### Judge prompt:

> Rate correctness from 1–10 and explain briefly.

### Output:

```text id="o1"
Score: 9
Reason: Correct and concise, minor missing detail about scale optimization.
```

---

# 3. Types of LLM-as-a-judge

---

## 3.1 Scalar scoring

Model outputs a score:

* 1–5
* 1–10
* 0–100

Used for:

* quality rating
* helpfulness
* correctness

---

## 3.2 Pairwise comparison (MOST RELIABLE)

Judge chooses between:

* Answer A vs Answer B

Example:

> Which is better?

✔ reduces bias
✔ more stable than absolute scoring
✔ widely used in production

---

## 3.3 Rubric-based evaluation

Judge follows strict criteria:

* correctness
* completeness
* clarity
* grounding
* safety

---

## 3.4 Multi-dimensional scoring

Instead of one score:

```json id="m1"
{
  "correctness": 9,
  "clarity": 8,
  "hallucination_risk": 2
}
```

# 5. Why it works

Because LLMs can approximate:

* reasoning patterns
* language quality judgment
* semantic similarity
* instruction adherence

So they act as a **proxy for human evaluators at scale**.

# 6. Advantages

---

## 6.1 Scalable human-like evaluation

✔ thousands of evaluations cheaply

---

## 6.2 Captures semantic quality

✔ better than BLEU/ROUGE for reasoning tasks

---

## 6.3 Flexible

Can evaluate:

* correctness
* style
* safety
* reasoning

---

## 6.4 Works for complex outputs

Especially useful for:

* RAG answers
* agent traces
* long-form generation

---

# 7. Limitations (very important)

---

## 7.1 Bias

Judge may prefer:

* longer answers
* certain styles
* model-specific phrasing

---

## 7.2 Inconsistency

Same input → different scores

---

## 7.3 Self-preference bias

If judge model is same family as generator, it may favor similar outputs.

---

## 7.4 Reward hacking risk

Models can be optimized to “look good to the judge” instead of being correct.

---

# 8. How production systems fix these issues

---

## 8.1 Calibration with humans

* sample outputs → human rating
* align judge scoring with humans

---

## 8.2 Pairwise ranking preferred over absolute scores

More stable signal.

---

## 8.3 Multi-judge ensemble

Use multiple judges:

* different models
* different prompts

Aggregate results.

---

## 8.4 Rubric standardization

Strict evaluation criteria reduces subjectivity.

---

## 8.5 Temperature control

Low temperature → more deterministic judgments.

# 10. Key use in regression detection

LLM-as-a-judge is heavily used for:

* prompt regression testing
* RAG quality scoring
* agent behavior evaluation

Because:

> human evaluation doesn’t scale, but rules don’t understand semantics

---

# 11. Key insight

> LLM-as-a-judge is a scalable approximation of human evaluation—but it must always be calibrated and validated against humans to remain reliable.

---

# 12. Senior-level interview answer

LLM-as-a-judge is an evaluation technique where a language model is used to assess the quality of outputs generated by another model or system. It is commonly used to score correctness, relevance, reasoning quality, and safety when ground truth or deterministic metrics are insufficient.

The judge model can provide scalar scores, pairwise preferences, or rubric-based multi-dimensional evaluations. This approach enables scalable, human-like evaluation for complex tasks such as RAG systems, agent trajectories, and long-form generation. However, it requires careful calibration with human judgments to mitigate bias, inconsistency, and reward-hacking risks.
::`
